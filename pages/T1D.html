Since 2009, I have been living with Type 1 Diabetes (T1D). Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune form of diabetes which often first shows itself in normally healthy children. Only about 5-10% of people with diabetes have type 1. With a little bit of planning and perseverance, T1D can be well managed.

T1D is also a great teacher of patience, persistence, diligence and attention to detail. Many people, including myself, have found diabetes to be a gift. Not that I would wish it on anyone, but it has taught me a lot. My T1D diagnosis started me looking for new activities and challenges leading me into robotics three weeks after my diagnosis. Robotics eventually led me to the field of computer science and where I am today.

I have compiled a list of frequently asked questions about how I have successfully managed attending university with T1D. I hope that this is helpful information. Best wishes!

Where do you keep all of your supplies while you are at school?

I always get a locker that is centrally located to my classes. I usually get a half-size locker.


I bought several tubs at the dollar store. I fill one tub with diabetes supplies, and the other two I fill with snacks such as cereal bars, trail mix, fruit bars and packs of cookies. I also bought a locker shelf from an office supply store, so I have room for supplies on top and school books or a coat on the bottom.


In my backpack, I keep basic supplies such as my tester and test strips, a spare insulin pen set, glucose tablets and a few snacks.




What supplies do you take to school?

Since I live about a 40 minute commute from school, I have a full stash of supplies in my locker. If you live closer to your school, you may not need as many supplies on hand. If you take a bit of time to plan ahead, you will have strong blood-sugar control, feel great and do your best in school.
Here is a list of the supplies I have at university:

In my locker:

  • 2-3 spare infusion sets
  • 2-3 spare pump insulin cartridges
  • Spare insulin
  • Extra batteries (tester and pump)
  • Spare test strips
  • Alcohol wipes
  • An insulin pen set for emergency use (if my infusion set fails, for example) with a small sticker with the start date. Pens should be changed out monthly.
  • Juice boxes
  • Glucose tablets
  • Medical tape (in case I need to tape my CGM sensor or infusion down)
  • Snacks (cereal bars, fruit bars, trail mix, cookies and so on)

How do you manage exams and diabetes?

As a student with T1D, there are many things that you can do to ensure your exams go well. I have found the following to be very helpful strategies:

  • Eat a healthy snack or meal before an exam. I ensure that I eat some carbohydrates for energy before a test, but not too many so my blood sugars go high. I also like to eat protein with the carbohydrate to help stabilize the carbohydrate’s effects on my blood. Chicken wraps work well for me.
  • Consider wearing a Continuous Glucose Monitor (CGM). I almost always wear a CGM, and I ensure that my CGM isn’t going to time-out during a test. I also plan to change sensors at least 1-2 days before a major exam, so they are well calibrated and accurate by the day of the test. I generally don’t check my blood during an exam as I can use my CGM to show my blood sugar levels as well as where my levels are trending. I have glucose tablets or food, if necessary, but I generally don’t find that I need to eat during an exam often.
  • Pack a baggie of supplies. Bringing in a clear baggie with supplies of low treatments, a blood tester and cereal bars to an exam is also a good idea. You should show these to your exam supervisor, so they know what is in the bag.
  • Look into registering with Accessibility Services. In the summer before my first year of university began, I registered with my university's Accessibility Services. Accessibility Services provides support to students with disabilities or medical conditions. Different schools have different names for this service. I needed to provide documentation from my endocrinologist confirming my diabetes. At the start of each semester, I go to Accessibility Services and pick up a letter for each of my professors explaining my diabetes. I then speak to each professor letting them know about my diabetes and having them sign the form which allows me to take exams at the exam center. I do not do all of my exams at the exam center as some require the use of specialized software which is only on the lab computers. I like taking the tests in the test center when possible as it is easy for the staff to supervise me while I am checking my blood or eating carbs, if necessary, but I have not had problems taking exams in the classrooms or labs as well. Where I chose to write my exam depends on the particular class and professor.

Pay attention to your health, study, prepare yourself, and you will be ready for your university exams.



I would like to use my continuous glucose monitor (CGM), but I keep getting bad rashes. Is there anything that I can do?

After wearing my CGM for about a year, I started getting a really bad rash that really hurt and burned. I found that if I cleaned the area where I wanted to put the sensor well with soap and water, instead of an alcohol wipe, it helped. I put a hydrocolloid pad over the clean, dry area. Hydrocolloid pads can be found in most drug stores and even grocery stores for around $1-$2 per pad. The sensor tape is too large for the hydrocolloid pad, so I trim off each end before I apply the sensor on top of the pad. The tape is what most people react to, so you don’t want it to stick over the pad touching your skin. I insert the sensor directly through the pad, and I have not had any issues doing it this way for the past two years. I then proceed to calibrate my sensor as per the instructions.

Wearing a CGM has been extremely helpful managing my diabetes while attending university. It has allowed me to accomplish everything I need to do in a day while having excellent control over my blood sugars.



How do you tell your professors that you have type 1 diabetes?

My professors have always been very supportive when I have told them that I have type 1 diabetes. Here is how I approach professors whom I need to speak to about my diabetes:

  • I wait until the end of the first lecture when they aren’t busy.
  • I show them the Accessibility Form when I am looking to write the class exams in the exam center, and I have them sign it.
  • I tell them that I have type 1 diabetes. I explain to them that because of the diabetes, I have to manage my blood sugar levels manually which I can do with my CGM and glucose tablets. I show them a new pack of glucose tablets, so they understand that it is like medicine to me not just a mid-class snack. If eating in class isn’t an issue, I don’t bother showing the glucose tablets.

I am thankful that my professors have always been kind, supportive and allowed me to use glucose tablets and/or eat in class.



How do you manage your time at school?

Managing your time is critical for success at school as well as in life. Here are some tips and photos to help you develop a time management system that works for you.

  • Make a weekly plan before the semester begins. This will give you a routine to get started at the beginning of the semester, so you don’t need to think about what to do or waste time trying to remember what you should study. Your plan is your guide.
  • Plan study times after each class as soon as possible to review the class material. Consider making a formula or condensed study sheet to help with your review times and studying for exams.   • Consider buying a large calendar where you can see your entire semester at a glance. I color code each of my subjects, and I write in my assignment due dates and exams at the very beginning of each semester.
  • I also take each of the due dates on my large calendar, and I put them into my online calendar which is part of my university account. I input each of my classes and other important information into this calendar. I also make use of the reminder/alert feature.
  • When I have a lot of assignments and exams, my schedule needs to be flexible. During midterms, finals and busy weeks, I use my weekly study plan as a guide, and I make a special plan for the week using a weekly planner. I try to stick to my weekly study schedule as much as possible, but it is important to be able to adapt to busy times or unusual weeks.
  • Remember, it is also important to plan time to eat a proper lunch in your schedule. This is important for everyone, but it is especially important for those managing diabetes.
  • Week-ends are my flex days. I do not have a regular plan for my week-ends other than some relaxation and sports with friends. I use the week-ends to study for exams, finish assignments, get ahead or work on challenging course topics. I make a plan at the beginning of each week-end.
  • Plan for break times, fun, time with friends and recreational activities. This will help you reduce stress, feel better and be healthy.
  • In addition to studying, relaxing and exercising, it is important to plan to take care of your diabetes. Consider reviewing your basil rates each week. Sunday evenings tend to work well for me. I also like to glance at my semester calendar and online calendar to check on important things happening during the week. This helps me decide when to change my CGM sensor and infusion sets.

If you plan ahead, diligently stick to your plan while being flexible, you will probably reduce your stress levels, be successful in school, manage your health well and have fun in the process!
